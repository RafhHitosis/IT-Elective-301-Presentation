Moving Online

Shopping has changed. We are moving from physical shops to buying things on our phones and computers.

  • Trillions of dollars are spent online every year.
  • Most people shop using their mobile phones.
  • It is not just about buying; it is about the experience.

Did you know?

Even when the economy is bad, online sales keep growing. It is the future of business.

Opportunities

The good things about going online.

Sell to More People

You are not limited by your city. A small shop can sell to customers in other countries easily.

  • Find new customers far away.
  • Sell special items to people who really want them.
  • Exporting goods is much easier now.
Example

ü¶å Gymshark

They started in a garage in the UK. Because of the internet, they could sell gym clothes to people all over the world instantly. Now they are a huge brand.

Save Money

Running an online store is cheaper. You don't need a fancy building or lots of staff standing around.

  • No Rent: You don't pay for a physical shop.
  • Smart Stock: Order items only when you sell them.
  • Less Staff: Computers handle the payments.
Business Idea

üì¶ Dropshipping

This is when you sell products you don't even touch. When a customer buys from you, the factory sends it directly to them. You hold zero stock.

Know Your Customer

Online, you can see exactly what people like. This helps you sell more effectively.

  • See what they look at.
  • Know when to order more stock.
  • Send them offers they actually like.
How it works

üé¨ Netflix Recommendations

Think about Netflix. It knows what movies you like and shows you more of them. Online stores do the same thing with products.

Open 24/7

Your store never sleeps. You can make money while you are in bed.

  • Sell to people in different time zones.
  • The store always looks perfect.
  • Customers can buy late at night.
New Tech

ü§ñ Smart Chatbots

Companies use AI "robots" to answer customer questions instantly, any time of day or night, without a human needing to be awake.

Better Interaction

Websites can do things physical stores can't. You can show videos, reviews, and interactive guides.

  • Video demos show how products work.
  • Customers can read reviews from others.
  • Building a fan community is easier.
Cool Feature

üíÑ Virtual Makeup

Some makeup apps let you use your phone camera to "try on" lipstick virtually. It's fun and helps you decide what to buy.

Easy to Grow

If your business gets popular suddenly, it's easy to handle. You don't need to build a bigger shop.

  • Handle big sales days like Black Friday.
  • Add thousands of new products instantly.
  • Change your website look in one click.
Big Event

üõçÔ∏è Black Friday

On Black Friday, millions of people shop at once. Online platforms can handle this huge crowd easily, whereas a real shop would be too full.

Personal Experience

Every customer is special. The website can change to match what each person likes.

  • Special prices for loyal customers.
  • Showing products based on history.
  • Emails that address you by name.
Brand

üëü Nike By You

Nike lets you design your own sneakers on their website. It makes you feel like the product is made just for you.

Risks

The dangers we need to watch out for.

Safety & Hackers

Criminals are online too. If they steal customer data, it can destroy a business.

  • Ransomware: Locking your computer for money.
  • Phishing: Tricking you into giving passwords.
  • DDoS: Attacking a site to shut it down.
Example

üè® MGM Hotel Attack

Hackers shut down a major hotel's computers. Guests couldn't even enter their rooms. It cost them millions of dollars.

Too Much Competition

Because it's easy to start, everyone is doing it. There is a lot of competition.

  • Competitors are just one click away.
  • It costs more to show ads now.
  • Big companies can sell things cheaper.
The Challenge

üî• Shein & Temu

These huge Chinese companies sell clothes very cheaply. It makes it very hard for small local businesses to compete with their prices.

Shipping Problems

Sending packages is hard and expensive. Customers want free and fast shipping.

  • Shipping costs are going up.
  • Customers return clothes that don't fit.
  • Packages can get lost or delayed.
Real Event

üö¢ Global Delays

A few years ago, ships were stuck at ports for weeks. Shops didn't get their products in time for Christmas, and they lost a lot of money.

Reliance on Tech

If the internet stops working, your store is closed. You rely heavily on other companies.

  • If the server breaks, you lose sales.
  • If Google changes rules, you might disappear.
  • If your account is banned, business stops.
Risk

‚òÅÔ∏è System Crash

Imagine if Amazon's servers crashed. Thousands of websites that use Amazon's technology would also stop working instantly.

Laws & Rules

Selling globally means following laws in many different countries. It can be confusing.

  • Privacy laws about customer data.
  • Different taxes in different countries.
  • Making sure the website works for disabled people.
Fines

üá™üá∫ Privacy Fines

Big companies like Facebook have been fined millions for not protecting user data properly. Even small sites need to be careful.

Trust Issues

Customers can't touch the product, so they have to trust you. If they don't trust you, they won't buy.

  • Bad reviews can hurt your sales.
  • People are scared of scams.
  • They worry their credit card info isn't safe.
Social Proof

‚≠ê Why Reviews Matter

If a product has a 4-star rating, it sells well. If it drops to 3 stars, sales can stop completely. Trust is everything.

Who is Involved?

The different players in E-commerce.

B2C (Business to Consumer)

This is the most common one. A business sells something to a person (you).

  • Buying a shirt, a book, or a phone.
  • It's usually a fast and easy purchase.
  • You buy what you like emotionally.
Example

üõí Amazon

When you buy a toy from Amazon for your child, that is B2C. The business (Amazon) sells to the consumer (You).

B2B (Business to Business)

This is when companies sell things to each other.

  • Buying 1000 computer chips or office software.
  • Contracts are bigger and take longer.
  • It's about logic and saving money.
Example

üè≠ Alibaba

A factory in China sells 5000 t-shirts to a clothing shop in New York. That is Business to Business.

C2C (Consumer to Consumer)

Regular people selling to other regular people. The website just helps them connect.

  • Selling used items you don't need.
  • Handmade crafts.
  • Trust between people is key.
Example

ü§ù eBay / Vinted

If you sell your old bike to someone in your town using an app, that is Consumer to Consumer.

C2B (Consumer to Business)

When a person provides value to a business.

  • Freelancers working for companies.
  • Influencers promoting a brand.
  • Answering surveys for money.
Example

üì∏ Freelancers

A photographer takes photos and sells that service to a marketing agency. The person serves the business.

Government & Administration

B2G

Business to Gov

Construction firms building roads or selling software to cities.

G2B

Gov to Business

Government auctions, selling permits, or large data services.

G2C

Gov to Consumer

Paying for parking tickets, renewing passports, or public info.

C2G

Consumer to Gov

Paying income taxes online, voting, or paying tuition for public school.
